What AI actually is (and isn't)

By the end of this page, you'll be able to explain in one plain sentence what you're actually talking to, and why it's fundamentally different from Google.

Most people you know who dismiss AI misunderstand what it is. Most people who are over-excited about it also misunderstand what it is. The truth is in the middle, and it's simpler than either side thinks.

The trick, in plain English

The AI you'll use in this course is called a large language model. The name is boring and doesn't help. Here's what it actually does, told without jargon.

Imagine a system that read almost everything ever written. Books, websites, conversations, manuals, recipes, poetry, code, legal briefs, casual chat. Then imagine that system learned one single skill really, really well: given some text, predict what text should come next.

That's the whole trick. That's what's happening every time you talk to ChatGPT or Claude. You type something, it predicts what should come next, and that prediction turns out to include answers to your questions, drafts of your emails, explanations of concepts, plans for your projects, and everything else you'll learn to get out of it.

The one-sentence version

AI is a very well-read assistant that predicts what helpful text should come next. Give it a good starting point, and what comes next is usually useful.

Why it feels like magic (and why it isn't)

People feel tricked the first time they use a good AI tool. "How can something that just predicts the next word also write a wedding speech, debug my code, and explain the French Revolution?" The answer is that if you've read enough human writing, good prediction looks exactly like intelligence. In most practical situations, the difference doesn't matter.

But the difference shows up in specific places, and that's important. Keep going.

How it's different from Google

Google looks up things that already exist. AI generates something new each time. That's the key difference, and it changes how you use it.

What AI is genuinely good at

  • Writing and rewriting. Emails, essays, posts, summaries, captions, cover letters, apologies, compliments. First drafts it produces in ten seconds are better than what most of us produce in ten minutes.
  • Explaining. You don't understand something? Ask it to explain. Still don't? Ask again, simpler. Still don't? Ask it to use an analogy from your favorite hobby. It will.
  • Brainstorming. Need ten ideas? Thirty? A hundred? It doesn't get tired. Doesn't get defensive about its ideas. Doesn't have opinions about your taste.
  • Organizing. Give it a mess of notes, it gives you structure. Give it a long document, it gives you a summary. Give it a decision, it gives you a framework.
  • Translating. Between languages, between tones, between levels of formality, between cultures.

What AI is genuinely bad at (in 2026)

  • Being sure about recent news. Most models don't know what happened last week unless they can search the web.
  • Knowing what it doesn't know. It will confidently give you wrong answers and sound just as sure as when it's right. This is called hallucination. We spend Day 5 on this.
  • Math and counting. Weirdly bad at exact numbers unless you ask it to reason step by step.
  • Doing things in the real world. On its own, AI only makes text. It can't send emails, make calls, or pay bills unless you connect it to other tools.
  • Being original. It's good at producing useful combinations of things it's already seen. It's not great at genuinely new ideas. Your taste, your experience, your weird angle, those are still yours to contribute.
The mental model to keep

Think of AI as a bright, incredibly well-read junior colleague who started last Monday. Smart. Helpful. Fast. Also: confident about things they shouldn't be. Needs clear direction. Needs you to double-check important work. Shouldn't be left alone with the nuclear launch codes.

Why this matters right now (and not five years from now)

For most of history, the biggest thing standing between people and opportunity was access. Access to education. Access to mentors. Access to the tools the rich already had. The internet cracked access to information. AI is cracking access to capability.

A teenager in a village with patchy wifi can now get the same writing coach, career coach, tutor, and thinking partner that a Harvard student has. Not a worse one. The same one. This is new. It's worth five minutes to let that sink in.

The people who figure this out early will spend the next decade compounding a massive advantage. That's not hype. That's just math.

How to talk to it and get real answers

The single skill that separates people who get generic AI output from people who get great AI output. Four things every good prompt includes, and one move that fixes most bad results.

Yesterday you sent your first messages. You probably noticed something: some of them got great responses, and some got replies that felt generic, wordy, or slightly off. Today you'll understand exactly why. And once you see it, you can't unsee it.

The root cause of every bad AI response

Almost every time AI gives you something disappointing, it's not because the tool is limited. It's because the prompt was vague. Vague questions produce vague answers. This isn't the AI's fault. Ask a random smart stranger "write me an email" and you'll get the same kind of generic, forgettable result.

The fix is not memorizing fancy prompt tricks. The fix is being specific about four things.

The four things every good prompt needs

1. Who you are

One sentence. Your role, your situation, what you actually do. A freelance graphic designer. A high school teacher. A father trying to plan his daughter's wedding. A small business owner in the cleaning industry. Whatever you are. This tells the AI who it's working for, which shapes everything about its response.

2. What you're actually trying to do

Not the task. The goal behind the task. "I want to follow up with a client who went quiet" is better than "write an email." "I want to understand this so I can explain it to my team" is better than "summarize this." The goal is what the AI is actually helping you achieve. The task is just the vehicle.

3. Who it's for

The audience. A customer. Your boss. Your mother-in-law. A five-year-old. A skeptic. A supporter. Every word the AI writes depends on who will read it.

4. What good looks like

Length. Tone. Format. Examples. Anything. "Short." "Formal." "Under 100 words." "Bullet points." "In the style of something I'd actually say." "Give me three options, not one." The more specific you are about what you want to see, the closer the output lands to what you actually need.

The formula, distilled

Who I am + what I'm trying to do + who it's for + what good looks like. If your prompt has all four, you're in the top 10% of AI users. Seriously. Most people skip two of them and wonder why the output is mediocre.

Watch the difference

Same task. Two prompts. Completely different output. The first one is what most people write. The second uses the four-part formula.

Write me an email to a client about a project.

You'll get a perfectly okay generic email. Could be from anyone to anyone. Now:

I run a small branding studio. I need to follow up with a client who went quiet after I sent a proposal two weeks ago. I don't want to sound pushy or desperate, but I also can't let this just die. Warm but professional. Under 100 words. Give me two options with slightly different tones.

Same ask, in plain language. You'll get two actually-useful options you can send with minor edits. The difference isn't clever words. It's just specificity.

The single trick that fixes 80% of bad results

Sometimes you don't know what you want until you see it. Fine. In that case, use this move:

Before you write anything, ask me 3 to 5 questions that would help you do this better.

This flips the effort. Instead of you trying to remember everything to include, the AI asks for what it needs. The answers you give become a much richer prompt than you would have written on your own. The output that comes next is dramatically better.

Try it on a task today. You'll use this move for the rest of your life.

Don't restart. Steer.

When the output isn't right, new users panic and start a whole new prompt. Don't. The AI remembers the conversation. Just tell it what to change.

  • "Shorter."
  • "Less formal."
  • "More like something I'd actually say."
  • "Make option 2 warmer."
  • "Take out the part about timelines."
  • "Rewrite it so it doesn't sound like a robot wrote it."

Any of these, by themselves, is a complete prompt. That's how conversations with AI actually work. You're not one-shotting instructions. You're steering.

The one habit to build today

After every AI response, ask yourself: is this close enough to ship, or do I need to steer? If you need to steer, one sentence is enough. Don't rewrite the whole prompt. Don't apologize. Just tell it what's wrong and keep going.

Context is the whole game

The biggest upgrade you'll make to how you use AI. Stop asking questions. Start handing it work.

You're past the awkward phase. You can send a clear prompt. You can steer a conversation. Today is where AI goes from mildly useful to genuinely useful. The difference is one word: context.

The move that matters most

Beginner users of AI ask questions. "How do I write a good business proposal?" "What's a good marketing strategy?" "How should I structure my week?" These questions get generic answers because the AI has nothing specific to work with. It's guessing who you are, what you have, what you need.

Experienced users of AI hand it work. "Here are my last three proposals. Here's the new client's request. Write a fourth proposal in the same voice." "Here's my business, here's my budget, here's my month's data, tell me what to focus on in May." "Here's my calendar for next week as a screenshot. Restructure it around this goal."

Same AI. Same tool. Completely different results. The pro version feeds it real information from real life. The AI does dramatically better when it's reacting to something specific than when it's generating from nothing.

The core idea

Generic input = generic output. Specific input = specific output. Every piece of real context you feed the AI doubles the usefulness of what comes back.

What counts as context

Any real-world material you give the AI about your actual situation. Examples:

  • The email thread you're trying to reply to. Paste the whole thing in.
  • Your old writing, so it can match your voice. Paste two or three samples.
  • A PDF of a contract, report, or document. Upload it.
  • A photo of a handwritten note, a whiteboard, a menu, a receipt, a document in another language. Upload it. Modern AI reads images now.
  • Your resume. The job description. Both at once.
  • Your spreadsheet. The meeting notes. The project brief. The product page. The complaint letter.
  • Your business, explained once, in a paragraph you can reuse.
One pattern you'll use forever

Most serious AI requests look like this: "Here's [the thing]: [paste or upload it]. Based on this, [what you want done]. Keep the [tone/length/format] consistent." That structure works for almost everything. Summarize. Rewrite. Critique. Respond. Improve. Translate. Extract.

The voice-matching trick

This one alone is worth the whole course. If you want AI to write in your voice, don't describe your voice. Show it.

Here are three examples of things I've written: [paste example 1] [paste example 2] [paste example 3] Study my voice. The rhythm, word choices, level of formality, what I avoid. Now write [new thing] in that same voice.

This works better than any amount of adjective gymnastics ("casual but professional, warm but direct, authoritative but humble..."). Examples beat adjectives every single time. You can save those three samples in a note and reuse them forever.

When it's wrong (and how to tell)

AI is wrong sometimes, and it sounds just as confident when it's wrong as when it's right. Today you learn when to trust it, when to check, and two habits that keep you safe.

This is one of the more important days in the course. AI tools are wrong more often than they look, and when they're wrong they sound just as confident as when they're right. Learning when to trust an answer and when to verify it is the difference between using AI well and getting caught out by it.

Meet the word you need to know: hallucination

When AI produces something that sounds plausible but is factually wrong, that's called a hallucination. A made-up quote. An invented statistic. A book that doesn't exist. A law that isn't real. A case that was never decided. A person who never said the thing it claims they said. A website link that 404s when you click it.

The tone will be just as confident as everything else the AI tells you. That's the trap. You can't tell from the writing whether it's right or wrong. You have to know when to check.

The rule to internalize right now

If the AI gives you a specific fact, number, quote, citation, name, date, or law, assume it might be wrong until you verify it. The more confident the tone on a specific detail, the more important it is to check.

When to trust AI more

  • Creative and subjective tasks. Drafts, brainstorming, tone adjustments. There's no "wrong" to hallucinate.
  • Reformatting what you gave it. Turning your notes into a plan, your bullet points into prose, your long thing into a summary. It's working with your material, not inventing.
  • Explaining concepts you can sanity-check. Things you partly know. You'll catch it if it veers off.
  • Discussing ideas. "What are the arguments on both sides of X." "What am I missing." "What would a skeptic say." These don't rely on specific facts.

When to trust AI less

  • Specific facts, stats, dates, names. Especially ones it states confidently without any hedge.
  • Legal, medical, financial, or tax advice. Useful as a first pass. Never the final word. Get a professional for anything consequential.
  • Recent events. Unless the AI has internet access and is actively searching, it may not know what happened yesterday, last month, or this year.
  • Quotes attributed to people. AI confidently invents quotes. Before you share any "Einstein said" or "Michelle Obama said" quote from AI, verify it.
  • Sources, links, citations, studies. These are hallucinated constantly. If you need a real source, make the AI find it with a web search, or go look it up yourself.
  • Math. Yes, really. Modern AI is much better, but still makes arithmetic errors. Ask it to show the steps.

Two habits that keep you safe

Habit 1: Ask it to show its work

For anything non-trivial, add one line to your prompt:

Walk me through your reasoning. What are you basing this on? What might you be wrong about?

This does two things. First, it often produces a better answer, because asking AI to think step by step makes it think step by step. Second, it exposes the weak spots. If it can't explain where an answer came from, that's a signal.

Habit 2: Ask it what it might be wrong about

After you get an answer, ask:

Before I use this, what assumptions did you make? What parts should I double-check with a real source? What might be outdated?

You'll be surprised how often the AI catches its own issues when you invite it to. It will often flag things like "the specific numbers are approximate, you should verify," or "this law varies by country, confirm your local version," or "I'm not certain this person said this exactly, look it up." That's gold.

The verify-or-ship test

Before you send, submit, publish, or rely on anything an AI produced, ask yourself one question: "If this is wrong, how bad is that for me?" If the answer is "who cares," ship it. If the answer is "I'd be embarrassed," "I'd lose the job," or "I'd get sued," verify first.

How to verify, fast

Checking doesn't have to take long. Three moves that cover most situations:

  • Google the quote or fact directly. Literally paste it into Google in quotes. If nobody has ever said it, that's telling.
  • Ask the AI to search. Most AI tools now have a web search mode. Turn it on and say "find me the actual source for this with a real link."
  • Check one thing, spot-check the rest. You don't have to verify every word. Check the most important claim, the most surprising claim, and any specific number. If those hold up, the rest probably does too.

What AI does well that feels risky but isn't

Some things feel risky to trust AI on, but actually aren't:

  • Tone and politeness. It has excellent instincts for how something will land.
  • Structure and flow. A paragraph re-ordered by AI is almost always more readable.
  • Spotting issues in your own writing. "What's weak about this argument" or "what would a skeptical reader push back on" works well.
  • Sanity-checking your plans. "What am I not considering about this decision" or "what would go wrong" are great uses.

These are judgment tasks, not fact tasks. Hallucination isn't really a factor.

The grown-up version of using AI

Here's the mature frame, which will serve you for decades: AI is a brilliant, fast, exhausting junior colleague. You don't fire them because they sometimes get things wrong. You don't blindly sign off on their work either. You review. You verify the important parts. You catch mistakes. You get vastly more done than you could alone, while staying the final authority on anything that matters.

That posture — collaborator, not oracle — is what keeps you safe and keeps you productive. Most people fall into one of two traps: total skepticism (never use it) or total trust (believe everything). The middle path is the gold.

The seven pieces of any real offer

Piece 1: The one-sentence pitch

If someone asks "what do you do?" and you can't answer in one sentence, you don't have an offer yet. The format that always works:

"I help [specific type of person] [specific outcome] in [specific timeframe or way]."

Bad: "I help people with AI." (Too vague. Why would anyone buy this?)

Good: "I help nurses in Texas rewrite their resumes so they get interviews at the hospitals they actually want."

Good: "I help small restaurants in Lagos respond to 50 Google reviews a month so they rank higher on maps."

Good: "I help job seekers in Houston who speak English as a second language get their cover letters to the top of the pile."

Piece 2: The price

Most people freeze on pricing and never start. Don't. Use these rules:

  • First 3 clients: charge half of what you'd eventually want to charge, or free in exchange for a testimonial and referrals.
  • After 3 clients with good results: charge your real starting price.
  • Every 5-10 clients: raise your price by 25-50%.

What actually differs between them

When comparing AI tools, these are the things that genuinely matter in daily use:

  • Voice. The tone and style of responses. Claude tends toward careful and nuanced. ChatGPT tends toward efficient and upbeat. Gemini tends toward balanced and sourced. Try both. Your ear will tell you which fits you.
  • How it handles long inputs. If you upload a 30-page PDF, does it actually read the whole thing or just the first part? Claude is consistently strongest here. Others catch up in specific versions.
  • Speed. Some tools respond faster. Most are close enough that this rarely matters.
  • Limits on the free tier. All have daily or per-hour message caps. Check the current ones when you sign up.
  • Features. Voice mode. Image generation. Web search. File uploads. Custom assistants. These come and go and change. Don't pick a tool based on a feature that might disappear next quarter.
  • Availability. Some tools don't work in some countries. Claude has the widest regional availability among the big three as of writing.

By task: who to use when

Writing long content (essays, articles, book chapters)

Claude first. Its voice is more distinct and less formulaic over long passages. If Claude isn't available, ChatGPT is solid. Gemini tends to feel flatter in long form.

Writing short content (emails, texts, posts, captions)

Any of the three work. ChatGPT is the fastest and most efficient for these. Claude is better if voice-matching matters. Gemini if you're already in Gmail.

Analysis of long documents (contracts, reports, textbooks)

Claude is the clear winner for documents over 20 pages. ChatGPT is fine for shorter ones. For anything you care about, double-check any specific facts or numbers.

Research questions with current info

Perplexity for research with real cited sources. Gemini for general current events (it's Google). ChatGPT with search mode on works too.

Thinking through a decision

Claude. It's more willing to disagree with you, ask hard questions, and resist flattery. Useful when you need an honest sparring partner rather than a cheerleader.

Image generation

ChatGPT (uses DALL-E) and Gemini both generate images. For higher-end creative work, Midjourney and Ideogram are dedicated tools worth learning separately.

Voice / conversation mode

ChatGPT has the most polished voice interface. Useful for on-the-go thinking, walking calls, and practice conversations.

Coding (if relevant to you)

Claude or ChatGPT. Both are excellent. Most developers report Claude edging ahead for complex code and refactoring.

The nine prompts that do the most work

These come from real operator use. They're longer than most prompts for a reason — they do heavier lifting. Paste them once, edit the bracketed parts, and watch the quality of the output jump. Start here.

Power Prompt 01The Output Analyzer

Use this right after the AI gives you any draft — an email, a plan, a post, a proposal, anything. It won't rewrite it. It'll tell you honestly what's weak, what's missing, and what would get picked apart by a smart reader. This is the single highest-leverage prompt on this page.

Analyze the output above deeply and act as a senior strategist, creative director, and high-converting copy expert. Identify anything missing, unclear, or off-brand, and call out weak spots or red flags. Anticipate objections a smart or skeptical reader might raise, and suggest how to overcome them. Make sure the tone, structure, and language align with the intended audience and purpose. Then provide clear, specific suggestions to make the final version more persuasive, polished, and bulletproof. If this had to go to a top-tier client, boss, teacher, or investor today, what would you improve before hitting send? Be thorough and brutally honest. Do not write the final output yet. I just want you to analyze and assess first.

Power Prompt 02The SOP Builder

Turn any task you do repeatedly into a step-by-step document someone else could follow. Great for handing off work, training a helper, or just making your own process consistent. Works for business tasks, household systems, study routines, anything.

Act like a world-class operations consultant who builds standard operating procedures for busy people and teams. Create a clear, step-by-step SOP for [INSERT TASK]. Break it down by owner, timing, tools needed, and expected outcomes. Include preconditions, quality checks, red flags to avoid, and specific real-world examples. The final output should be so clear and repeatable that a brand-new hire (or a family member, or a future version of me) could follow it with zero training and still get the job done right.

Power Prompt 03The Meeting Recap

Paste in notes, a transcript, or both from any meeting, class, or important conversation. Get back a clean summary you can send to others or save for yourself. Works for work meetings, school lectures, doctor visits, anything you want to remember properly.

Act as a Chief of Staff and expert meeting strategist. I'm going to paste in the notes and/or transcript from a meeting I was in. Use everything I give you to generate a clear, detailed summary I can share with others or keep for myself. The summary should include: A short intro line (1-2 sentences) on what the meeting covered A "Takeaways & Decisions" section that bullet-points the major insights, decisions, and high-level updates A "Next Steps" section broken down by person, listing the specific action items each person is responsible for (including deadlines if mentioned or inferred) A reminder of the next meeting date/time if discussed (otherwise say "TBD") Write it in a friendly but professional tone, formatted cleanly with bold section headers and bullets where helpful. [Paste notes and/or transcript here]

Power Prompt 04The Post-Conversation Follow-Up

After any important call, meeting, or in-person conversation where there's a "next step" to be taken. This prompt writes a warm, direct follow-up that references real details from the conversation, clearly lays out what's next, and removes friction. Based on Alex Hormozi's value equation. Turns a vague idea like "I want to sell X" into a structured, compelling offer people actually want to buy. Works for any service, product, or proposal — business, freelance, community, anything you're trying to get people to say yes to.

Plain-English glossary

Every AI term you'll see, defined without jargon. If you hear a word you don't know, look it up here first.

AI (Artificial Intelligence)Computer systems that do things we used to think only humans could do: read, write, understand, make decisions. The word covers everything from a recommendation algorithm to ChatGPT. Broad and a little vague.
AgentAn AI that can take action on its own, not just produce text. Sends emails, books flights, runs searches. Still early-stage technology in 2026. Useful but imperfect.
API (Application Programming Interface)A way for other software to talk to an AI. Only relevant if you're building tools. Ignore if you're not a developer.
ChatGPTThe AI chat product from OpenAI. The most widely known AI tool. Based on the GPT models.
ClaudeThe AI chat product from Anthropic. Known for thoughtful long-form writing and document analysis.
Context windowHow much text the AI can pay attention to at once. Bigger context window means you can upload longer documents and have longer conversations without the AI "forgetting" earlier parts. Grows every year.
Custom GPT / Project / GemA saved, specialized version of the AI preloaded with your context and instructions. You build one once and reuse it for recurring work. ChatGPT calls them GPTs, Claude calls them Projects, Gemini calls them Gems.
Fine-tuningAdvanced technique of training a custom version of an AI model on your own data. Expensive and overkill for almost everyone. Forget this word for now.
GeminiGoogle's AI chat product. Integrated into Gmail, Docs, and other Google tools.
Generative AIAI that produces new content (text, images, audio, video) as opposed to just classifying or searching existing content. Includes all the tools in this course.
GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer)The family of AI models OpenAI builds. The current generation is GPT-5, which comes in versions like GPT-5.3 Instant (the default), GPT-5.4 Thinking, and GPT-5.4 Pro. When people say "GPT" they usually mean ChatGPT.
HallucinationWhen AI produces something that sounds right but is factually wrong. Made-up facts, invented quotes, fake sources. The #1 thing to watch for. See Day 5.
Input / PromptWhat you type into the AI. A prompt is just an input with an intent behind it.
LLM (Large Language Model)The specific kind of AI that powers ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini. Trained by reading huge amounts of text. Very good at predicting what text should come next, which turns out to be remarkably useful.
MemoryA feature where the AI remembers things about you across different conversations. You can turn it on, off, or edit what it remembers.
ModelA specific version of an AI, like Claude Opus 4.7, Claude Sonnet 4.6, or GPT-5.4. Different models have different strengths — some are faster, some are smarter, some are cheaper. See the "Which model to use" reference page for how to pick.
MultimodalAn AI that handles multiple types of input (text, images, audio, video) in the same conversation. Most modern AI is now multimodal.
Open sourceAI models whose underlying code and weights are free to download and modify. Llama, Mistral, DeepSeek are examples. Running them usually requires technical skill.
OutputWhat the AI gives back after you send a prompt. Also called "response" or "completion."
ParametersInternal numbers inside the AI model. More parameters usually means more capability, but not always. Mostly a technical detail. Ignore unless you're geeking out.
Plugin / Extension / ConnectorAn add-on that lets the AI connect to another tool (your calendar, your drive, your email). Powerful but still rough around the edges.
PromptWhat you type into the AI to get a response. Your instructions, question, or request. The quality of your prompt heavily determines the quality of what comes back. See Day 3.
Prompt engineeringThe skill of writing good prompts. This whole course is basically teaching it without using the word.
Prompt libraryYour personal collection of saved prompts that have worked well. See Day 6.
RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)A technique where the AI looks things up in a specific set of documents before answering. Ignore unless you're building tools.
Reasoning modelA newer kind of AI that "thinks" step-by-step before answering, rather than responding instantly. Slower but better for complex problems. ChatGPT's "Thinking" mode and Claude's "extended thinking" are examples. See the "Which model to use" reference page.
System promptA behind-the-scenes instruction that sets the AI's overall behavior. You don't usually see this. Developers use it to customize AI products.
TemperatureA setting that controls how random the AI's output is. Higher = more creative and varied. Lower = more consistent and predictable. You rarely need to touch this.
TokenThe little chunks of text AI reads in. Roughly 3-4 characters each. You pay or hit limits in tokens, not words. Not something you need to manage in daily use.
TrainingThe process of teaching the AI by feeding it huge amounts of text and letting it learn patterns. Already done by the company that made the model. You don't train the AI by chatting with it.
Training cutoff / Knowledge cutoffThe date past which the AI doesn't know about events. Most models don't know what happened in the last few months unless they have web search turned on.
Vibe codingA casual term for using AI to write code by describing what you want in plain language. You don't have to know how to code to get useful code out. Powerful and increasingly common.
Zero-shot / Few-shotJargon for "asking the AI to do something without examples" (zero-shot) vs "giving it a few examples first" (few-shot). The second works way better. Called "the voice-matching trick" in Day 4.

What to do after Day 10

The course is over. The work starts here. This page is the map for the next 90 days and beyond, in rough order of usefulness.

The only thing that matters: keep going

The single most valuable thing you can do after this course is simple and boring: use AI once a day, for real work, for the next 30 days. That's the whole practice. Everything below is optional flavor. The daily habit is the main dish.

The minimum

Daily: one real task with AI. Weekly: 30 minutes of setup maintenance. That's it. Everything else is extra credit.

The first 30 days after the course

A suggested rhythm if you want one:

Week 1: Consolidate

  • Use the prompts from your library daily. Refine the ones that work.
  • Paste your about-me block at the start of every new chat. Build the muscle.
  • If you did Day 9, follow up with any silent outreaches. Deliver any accepted ones.
  • Don't try anything new this week. Just cement what you have.

Week 2: Expand

  • Try AI on one task you haven't used it for yet. A kind of writing you avoid, a decision you're sitting on, a document you've been putting off.
  • Try a second AI tool (if you've only used one) on the same task. Notice the difference.
  • Add 2-3 new prompts to your library based on what you tried.

Week 3: Depth

  • Build your first Custom GPT / Project / Gem. Preload it with a role and your about-me block. Use it for one recurring kind of task.
  • If you're doing paid work, raise your prices by 25% for new clients.
  • Write down what you've learned about how you work with AI. Keep the note.

Week 4: Teach

  • Teach one person what you've learned. A friend, a family member, a colleague. Teaching is the best way to cement your own understanding.
  • Notice what you can explain easily and what you can't. The gaps are where to study next.
